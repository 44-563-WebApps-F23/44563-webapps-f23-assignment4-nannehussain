An Enemy of the People , Henrik Ibsen

ACT-IV Scene.-

Horster:

I thought you might all sit here; you can slip out easily from here, if things get too lively.

(One can never tell—with such a crowd. But sit down, and don’t be uneasy).

Mrs Stockmann:

It was extremely kind of you to offer my husband the room.

(Looks at his watch, steps on to the platform, and bows. It is a quarter past—so I will begin. (Takes his MS. out of his pocket)

Peter Stockmann:

I certainly think too that we ought to have a chairman.

(PETER STOCKMANN makes his way gradually through the crowd, bows courteously, and takes up a position by the wall on the left. Shortly afterwards Dr. STOCKMANN comes in by the right-hand door. He is dressed in a black frock-coat, with a white tie. There is a little feeble applause, which is hushed down. Silence is obtained.)

Aslaksen:

And now, as I am in this position, I should like to say a few brief words. I am a quiet and peaceable man, who believes in discreet moderation, and—and—in moderate discretion. All my friends can bear witness to that.

(ringing his bell). Silence, please, gentlemen! I beg to support the Mayor's motion. I quite agree with him that there is something behind this agitation started by the Doctor.

Hovstad:

I, too, feel called upon to explain my position. Dr. Stockmann's agitation appeared to be gaining a certain amount of sympathy at first, so I supported it as impartially as I could. But presently we had reason to suspect that we had allowed ourselves to be misled by misrepresentation of the state of affairs

(It is an insult to a respectable population!)

Dr Stockmann:

I have thought and pondered a great deal, these last few days—pondered over such a variety of things that in the end my head seemed too full to hold them

(Laughter, uproar and hisses. MRS. STOCKMANN Sits coughing assiduously. ASLAKSEN rings his bell violently.)

Angry Voices:

Turn out that drunken man! Turn him out.slaksen. I think we ought to elect a chairman first.

(He is turned out again.)
